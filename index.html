1 Nyquist–Shannon Sampling Theorem

  • Deﬁnes the requisites of the sampling process to be sucessful [2, 1].
  • Let s(t) a band-width limited signal, with maximum frequency component fm Hz. The Sampling Theorem says that, in order to sample s(t), 2fm samples per second must taken. 2fm is known as the Nyquist Frequency.

Proof

  • According to the statement, we have that S(ω) = 0 when |ω| > ωm (it is limited in band), where ωm = 2πfm is the maximum angular frequency of s(t).
  • The sampling process of s(t) can be modeled mathematically by the multiplication of s(t) with a train of unit impulses δTs(t).
  • Let
    ss(t) = s(t)δTs(t) (1)

    the resulting sampled signal, where (see deﬁnition of δT in Fourier Transform of train of equidistant unit impulses (δT(t)))

    δTs(t) = ∑ n=−∞∞δ(t − nTs) (2)

    and

    Ts = 2π ωs (3)

    is the sampling period, being

    ωs = 2πfs (4)

    the angular sampling frequency, expresed in radians/second.

  • As we also known from Fourier Transform of train of equidistant unit impulses, that:
    ℱ[δTs(t)] = ΔTs(ω) = ωsδωs(ω). (5)

    Considering that the multiplication of two functions in the time domain is equivalent to the convolution of their spectrums, it holds that

    Ss(ω) = 1 2πS(ω) ∗ ωsδωs(ω). (6)

    After substituting ωs = 2π Ts, we get that

    Ss(ω) = 1 TsS(ω) ∗ δωs(ω) (7)

    which by deﬁnition of δωs(ω), is

    = 1 TsS(ω) ∗∑ n=−∞∞δ(ω − nωs) (8)
    = 1 Ts ∑ n=−∞∞S(ω) ∗ δ(ω − nωs) (9)
    = 1 Ts ∑ n=−∞∞S(ω − nωs). (10)

    Therefore, the spectrum of ss(t) is equal to the replica of the spectrum of s(t) every ωs radians/seconds.

  • As it can be seen, it is possible to reconstruct S(ω) from Ss(ω) if the frequencies higher than ωm are discarded (ﬁltered out), multipliying the spectrums
    S(ω) = Sm(ω)TsG2ωm′(ω) (11)

    where the rectangular signal is

    TsG2ωm′(ω) = Ts if |ω| < ωm′ 0 otherwise (12)

    provided that

    ωm ≤ ωm′.

  • TsG2ωm′(ω) is the Fourier transform is the Sinc signal
    2Tsωm′Sinc(ωm′ω) (13)

    which is, by deﬁnition, the response of a low-pass ﬁlter, with cut-oﬀ frequency ωm′ and gain Ts, to the unit impulse, also known as the transfer function of the ﬁlter. Therefore, it also holds that

    s(t) = ss(t) ∗ 2Tsωm′Sinc(ωm′ω). (convolution_sampling)
  • The eﬀect produced with ωm′ < ωm is known as aliasing.

    In this situation, it is impossible to reconstruct s(t) from ss(t) because S(ω) can not be extracted from Ss(ω).

2 Time-limited (ﬁnite) sampled signals have frequency-unlimited (inﬁnite) spectrums

  • Lets suppose fm is the maximum frequency of the signal s(t).
  • A ﬁnite signal s′(t) can be obtained from s(t) by multiplying:
    s′(t) = s(t)w τ(t) (time-domain_windowing)

    where wτ(t) (that in its simplest shape is a rectangular signal) is commonly called, *Window signal* or *Window function*.

  • Considering the convolution theorem, Eq. (time-domain_windowing) equals to:
    S′(ω) = S(ω) ∗ W τ(ω) (frequency-domain_windowing)

    where Wτ(ω) is (again, in its simplest shape) a Sinc signal, that by deﬁnition if bandwidth unlimited (except if the duration of the window is inﬁnite).

3 Minimizing the spectral leakage

  • The shape of Wτ(ω) aﬀects to the shape of the windowed signal S′(ω): the higher the resemblance between Wτ(ω) and a Delta signal, the lower the deformation of S′(ω) compared to S(ω)
  • Some famous Window functions are the follow:
    1. Rectangular Window:
      wτ(t) = 1 if |t| < τ 2 0 otherwise (14)
    2. Hanning Window:
      wτ(t) = 0.5 − 0.5cos(2πt) if |t| < τ 2 0 otherwise (15)
    3. Bartlett (triangular) Window
      wτ(t) = 2t if  −τ 2 < t ≤ 0 − 2t if 0 < t ≤ τ 2 0 otherwise (16)
